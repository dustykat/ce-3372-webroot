Design of Water Supply Systems#

Designing a drinking water supply and treatment system is one of the most complex and rewarding tasks in civil and environmental engineering. A successful design balances technical performance, public health protection, cost, and sustainability. Engineers must consider interacting factors such as demand estimation, water source protection, treatment processes, storage capacity, and distribution network configuration to ensure that a community’s water supply remains safe, reliable, and resilient over time.

The design process is guided by established manuals and standards, including the American Water Works Association (AWWA) Manuals of Practice, the Ten States Standards, and various state or municipal design guides. These resources provide consistent frameworks for planning, design, and construction — covering everything from pipe sizing and pump selection to treatment plant hydraulics and system redundancy.

Design as the Management of Constraints#

Design is the management of constraints — balancing what must happen with what can happen.

  • Non-negotiable constraints are dictated by physics, chemistry, and regulation.
    Water flows downhill unless energy is supplied to lift it; disinfectant residuals decay unless replenished; pressure cannot be maintained without sufficient head.

  • Negotiable constraints are variables engineers can adjust — such as materials, layout, timing, budget, or aesthetics — to achieve performance within those physical limits.

In short, the designer’s role is to meet system needs by manipulating the negotiable variables to satisfy the non-negotiable requirements.
Analytical tools and computer models allow engineers to test designs virtually before construction begins — reducing uncertainty, avoiding costly field errors, and ensuring that the final design meets both technical and regulatory standards.

Hydraulic Principles in Design#

Hydraulics governs how water moves through pipes, pumps, tanks, and treatment processes. Sound hydraulic design ensures that adequate pressure and flow are maintained throughout the system — from the source, through the treatment plant, to the customer’s tap.

Key Principles#

  • Flow and Pressure: Equations such as Bernoulli’s and Manning’s help determine head loss, pipe sizes, pump capacities, and tank elevations. Typical pressures range from 40–80 psi.

  • Energy and Grade Lines: The Hydraulic Grade Line (HGL) helps visualize changes in energy along a system. Gravity flow, when possible, minimizes pumping and lowers operating costs.

  • System Optimization: Modeling software such as EPANET or WaterGEMS allows simulation of network performance, including transient behavior and water age.

In treatment plants, hydraulics ensures that each stage — sedimentation, filtration, or chemical dosing — operates effectively at design flow rates.
Designers also account for transient effects such as water hammer by using surge tanks, air-release valves, or slow-closing valves to protect system components.

Design Fundamentals for Water Systems#

All water system designs begin with four essential questions:

  1. How much water will be used?
    Estimate current and future demands, including peak and fire-flow conditions.

  2. Where are the supply sources located?
    Identify groundwater wells, surface intakes, desalination plants, or rainwater systems — each has distinct implications.

  3. Where are the consumption points?
    Map demand centers to determine pressure zones, storage needs, and pump requirements.

  4. How does demand vary with time?
    Analyze diurnal and seasonal variations to size reservoirs and schedule pumping efficiently.

Note

Building a modern database of local demand data would make an excellent data-science project (ENGR 1330).

Transmission and Distribution Networks#

Transmission mains carry large volumes of water from sources or treatment plants to storage facilities.
Distribution mains deliver treated water to consumers through smaller interconnected pipes.

Common Layouts#

  • Tree (Branched) Systems:
    Simple and economical; often used in rural areas but prone to stagnation and service disruption.

  • Grid or Loop Systems:
    Interconnected networks providing better pressure stability, redundancy, and water quality — preferred in most urban designs.

  • Arterial Loop Systems:
    Large backbone mains surrounding the service area, supplying smaller grids from multiple directions.

Note: Arterial loop and grid systems are functionally similar — terminology varies by locality.

A well-designed system also includes appurtenances — valves, hydrants, blow-offs, and air-release devices — that aid operation and maintenance.

Design Guidance and Professional Standards#

Designers rely on three main categories of information:

1. Regulatory Documents#

Federal and state agencies, such as the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and state environmental departments, issue regulations governing system design, construction, and operation.
States may adopt more stringent rules than the federal minimums.

2. Design Manuals#

Local governments publish design manuals that define acceptable materials, construction practices, and testing procedures.

Representative examples:

  • Washington State Water System Design Manual (2018)

  • City of Houston Design Manual

  • City of Lubbock Design Manual

  • City of San Marcos Design Manual

Always use the manual specific to your project’s jurisdiction — requirements and standards are updated frequently.

3. Professional and Manufacturer Literature#

For unusual or innovative projects, engineers may consult manuals of practice, vendor specifications, or peer-reviewed research.
Manuals and vendor data are generally preferred because they reflect proven, field-tested technologies and reduce design risk.

Project Layout (Concept to Schematic)#

Most design manuals devote space to submittal and approval requirements for drawings. Within the right-of-way, however, the physical layout is largely a hydraulic design choice.

A typical early-stage workflow:

  1. Prepare a base map and sketch a grid for the service area (scaled aerials help).

  2. Place nodes (junctions) and assign average demands.

  3. Apply peak factors and fire-flow requirements.

  4. Add future growth allowances.

  5. Test and iterate with a hydraulic model.

Why nodes matter. Models compute pressure, demand, water quality, and velocity at nodes against jurisdictional criteria (min/max values are in local guidance). Node spacing and exact placement are flexible on grids but matter more on transmission mains and in areas with steep grades.

Practice note: You can design without software, but modern free tools (e.g., EPANET) are accurate and expected by many jurisdictions. Commercial suites often integrate better with drafting/asset tools and can repay their license in time savings.

Existing Data (What You Need to Lay Out a Network)#

  • Demand inputs: Confirm with the owner; use land-use/zoning and local records for current and projected demands.

  • Map-based compilation: Put demands on the base map; this drives node placement and preliminary pipe diameters.

Pipe Diameter (Concept Rules)#

  • Trench/installation is the major cost—size for hydraulics, not just material price.

  • Meet peak and fire-flow while maintaining adequate dynamic pressure.

Pressure Zones (When Terrain Drives Design)#

Large elevation differences require zones to avoid excessive low-area pressures and inadequate high-area pressures. As a rule of thumb, > ~80 ft elevation difference may trigger zoning.

Hydraulic grade line checks:

[ \mathrm{HGL_{min}} = \text{Highest Elev.} + 2.31 \times \text{Min. Pressure (psi)} ]

[ \mathrm{HGL_{max}} = \text{Lowest Elev.} + 2.31 \times \text{Max. Pressure (psi)} ]

def hglmin(pressure,elevation):
    hglmin = elevation+2.31*pressure
    return(hglmin)
    
def hglmax(pressure,elevation):
    hglmax = elevation+2.31*pressure
    return(hglmax)
    
pmin = 30 # pressure in psi (about 2 atm)
pmax = 90 # pressure in psi (about 6 atm)
emin = 5 # low elevation in feet
emax = 95 # high elevation in feet

print(hglmin(pmin,emax))
print(hglmax(pmax,emin))

Junctions (Placement Hints)#

  • Follow the site plan first; refine for hydraulics.

  • In rolling terrain, set nodes near low points of loops; in flat grids, spacing affects operations more than steady-state hydraulics.

Materials (Fit for Purpose)#

  • Common choices: Ductile Iron, PVC, HDPE, ABS.

  • Select by pressure class, soil, constructability, and local standard.

  • Always check the local design manual for approved materials.

Important Considerations for Distribution Design (At Concept Stage)#

Aging U.S. networks (many pipes ≈ 45 + years old) and growing communities require targeted upgrades. Early decisions should reflect the factors below:

Factor

Why it matters

How it shapes design

Age/condition

Older assets fail more; leakage and breaks waste treated water

Prioritize renewal and looping; phase replacements

Geography & climate

Topography, soils, freeze depth affect siting/materials

Set zone elevations, cover depth, and material class

Demand profile

Average, peak, and fire flow drive capacity

Size pipes/pumps/storage for ADD, PHD, fire flow

Regulations

Quality, separation, permits vary by state/city

Design to local manuals; meet state/federal rules

Budget & funding

Scope follows funding; O&M must be sustainable

Phase projects; pursue SRF/grants; optimize life-cycle

Growth/expansion

Population and land-use change over time

Provide expandability and standard pipe sizes

Layout inputs typically include: flows, pressures, velocities, fire flows, topography, utilities, materials, ordinances, right-of-way limits, environmental constraints, and budget. Field-operator input is invaluable for maintainability and constructability.

Schematic workflow (condensed):

  1. Base grid on map

  2. Assign demands

  3. Add peaks + fire flow

  4. Place storage/pumps

  5. Simulate

  6. Refine sizes/zones/controls

  7. Select the feasible (hydraulic + economic) alternative

Software-Aided Design (Model, Check, Iterate)#

Network simulators solve the hydraulics for all pipes, nodes, tanks, pumps, and controls.

EPANET (free, EPA) tracks flows, pressures, tank levels, and water quality/age.
It supports:

  • Hazen–Williams, Darcy–Weisbach, and Chezy–Manning head-loss formulas

  • Minor losses for fittings and bends

  • Fixed or variable-speed pumps

  • Any tank shape and flexible control rules

Typical modeling cycle:

  • Build: sources, storage, pipes, nodes, elevations, demands

  • Run scenarios: peak hour (check min pressure), low demand (check max pressure), extended period (controls, water age)

  • Review: low-pressure nodes, high-headloss pipes, storage balance

  • Adjust: sizes, setpoints, elevations, or controls; re-run until criteria met

  • Report: assumptions, criteria, results, and final configuration (often required for permits and funding)

Workflow Summary (From Map to Preferred Alternative)#

  1. Base mapping — surveys, contours, imagery, and field verification

  2. Demand projection — average, max-day, peak-hour, and fire flow

  3. Tentative layout — sources, storage, zones, mains along ROW

  4. Demand distribution — assign to nodes or sub-areas

  5. Data encoding — pipe lengths/diameters/roughness; node elevations/demands

  6. Hydraulic simulation — steady-state + extended-period analyses

  7. Result review — pressures, velocities, headloss, and storage operation

  8. Refinement — adjust sizes, controls, and elevations

  9. Select configuration — meets design criteria and budget

  10. Document — results for permits, funding, and design development

Connecting Design to Documentation#

Design is not complete until it can be clearly communicated.
The next two stages — Project Reports and Construction Documents — transform design intent into structured, reviewable documentation.

  • The Project Report explains why the design was developed, presenting supporting data and engineering justification.

  • The Construction Documents show how the design will be built, detailing materials, layout, and testing procedures.

Together, these deliverables bridge the gap between conceptual design and real-world construction, ensuring that each community’s water system is not only well-conceived on paper but also safely and effectively realized in the field.

Project Reports#

Engineering projects must be clearly communicated so that others can understand, review, and build upon the work. The project report is the primary way engineers do this. It explains the purpose of a project, presents the technical reasoning behind design choices, and documents how the design meets performance, safety, and regulatory requirements.

For future engineers, learning how to organize and write a clear project report is a valuable professional skill. It combines technical accuracy, logical structure, and clear communication—three traits that distinguish effective engineers.

A project report includes the calculations, assumptions, and design criteria needed to demonstrate that a proposed design will achieve its goals safely and efficiently. Engineers typically prepare the project report before producing detailed construction drawings. The content should reflect accepted engineering practices, and the amount of detail should match the complexity and importance of the project.

This section introduces the essential components that appear in most project reports. For specific projects, you should always check the relevant design manuals and the regulatory requirements of the jurisdiction where the project will be built and operated.

Project Description#

The project description explains what the project is about and why it is being done. A clear, accurate description helps reviewers understand the project’s purpose, ensures efficient regulatory review, and becomes part of the project’s permanent design record.

Most project reports should summarize the following points:

The reason for the project and the problems it aims to solve.

The recommended design alternative, proposed construction schedule, estimated cost, and funding source.

The relationship between this project and other parts of the water system.

How the project will change system capacity or improve service.

Environmental documentation, such as an impact statement or an explanation of why environmental review does not apply.

Information about source development, if the project involves a new or modified water source.

The type of treatment process proposed, if applicable.

Planning#

Good planning connects engineering design to real-world needs and regulations. It ensures that the project aligns with community goals, available resources, and long-term system requirements.

If no current plan addresses these issues, the project report should include:

Background information, including projected population and water demand.

A service area map showing the retail and general service areas.

Discussion of how the project may affect neighboring water systems.

Local requirements, such as minimum fire flow and duration.

Management and certification needs (e.g., Water Works Operator requirements).

A schedule for project implementation, including major phases.

Capital and operating cost estimates with potential financing methods.

A Water Rights Assessment, if applicable.

Analysis of Alternatives#

An analysis of alternatives compares different ways to meet the project objectives. This step helps ensure that the final design is efficient, reliable, and cost-effective.

Projects that skip this step often face higher costs, reduced reliability, or compliance problems later. Engineers should match the chosen design to the system’s capabilities—balancing performance, maintainability, and affordability.

Common evaluation factors include life-cycle cost, operating reliability, ease of maintenance, and implementation time.

Water Quality#

Water quality protection is central to all water system design. Engineers must consider how each design decision affects public health and delivered water quality.

The project report should include:

Results of water quality testing from accredited laboratories, covering both raw and treated water.

A discussion of how the proposed project will maintain or improve quality.

Pilot testing methods, if the project includes treatment or filtration.

Designers must think holistically: fixing one water quality issue (such as taste, color, or bacteria) should not create new ones (such as corrosion or disinfection byproducts).

Examples of unintended effects include:

Adjusting pH or adding chlorine in ways that increase corrosion and raise lead or copper levels.

Adding a new source with higher pH that causes iron or manganese precipitation.

Introducing a source with organic matter that leads to more disinfection byproducts after treatment.

Engineering Calculations#

Engineering calculations demonstrate how design decisions are supported by data and sound reasoning. These calculations allow reviewers to verify compliance with design standards.

A project report typically includes:

Capacity analyses showing that facilities can handle design flows.

Hydraulic analyses of pipelines, pumps, or reservoirs.

Sizing justifications for all key components.

Including references to supporting data or appendices helps reviewers follow the design logic without unnecessary repetition.

Design Criteria#

Design criteria define the standards, assumptions, and minimum requirements that guide the project. Listing these criteria ensures that both designers and reviewers understand the basis for every major decision.

Examples include:

Design and construction standards, including materials, performance goals, and control systems.

Locally adopted requirements, such as fire flow rates and minimum system pressures.

Water Rights and Legal Considerations#

Legal aspects such as water rights and land ownership can determine whether a project is even feasible. Projects that increase water withdrawals, add new sources, or expand service areas often require a Water Rights Assessment.

If an assessment shows that the project would exceed permitted withdrawal limits, the design must be modified to stay within legal constraints.

The project report should also document other legal factors, such as:

Land ownership and rights-of-way.

Sanitary control areas.

Deed restrictions or covenants affecting water use.

Operations and Maintenance (O&M) Considerations#

Projects that add new facilities—such as reservoirs, pumps, or treatment units—also add operation and maintenance responsibilities. The project report should explain how the system will manage these new duties.

Include:

Routine operating tasks and their frequencies.

Preventive maintenance activities.

Annual cost estimates (labor, power, equipment, materials).

Life-cycle cost summaries.

Discussion of operator certification needs or the potential need for a satellite management agency if a new water system is created.

Environmental Policy Requirements#

Before construction begins, many projects must show compliance with environmental policy laws. Depending on the jurisdiction, this may require:

An Environmental Impact Statement (EIS),

A Determination of Non-Significance (DNS), or

Documentation explaining why environmental review does not apply.

These requirements generally apply to:

New surface water source developments.

Large storage facilities (e.g., greater than 0.5 million gallons).

Long or large transmission lines (e.g., more than 1,000 feet and 12 inches in diameter in a new right-of-way).

Major system extensions that increase the service area by more than one square mile.

Understanding and planning for these requirements early helps prevent costly delays later in the project.

Why Project Reporting Matters

Learning to prepare a professional project report helps you think like an engineer. The process brings together many of the abilities expected of practicing professionals: identifying problems, applying technical knowledge, evaluating alternatives, and communicating results clearly and responsibly.

Good project reporting also reflects ethical practice—being transparent about data, assumptions, and limitations—and supports sustainable, safe, and compliant designs. These skills align closely with the ABET student outcomes, especially those related to effective communication, consideration of safety and ethics, teamwork, and lifelong learning.

In short, mastering the structure and purpose of project reports now will make you better prepared for upper-division design courses, internships, and the responsibilities of professional engineering practice.

Construction Documents

Once a project report is completed and approved, engineers prepare construction documents—the detailed drawings and written specifications that tell builders exactly how to create the proposed infrastructure. These documents translate design concepts into buildable reality.

Construction documents must show how each part of the project will be constructed to meet the requirements and conditions established in the approved project report. Together, they form a legal and technical record of the design intent.

All construction documents should follow the standards of professional engineering practice, including accuracy, clarity, and completeness. Approval of construction documents is typically valid only for a limited period (for example, two years). If construction takes longer, the design engineer must request an extension before the approval expires. Regulatory agencies may apply updated design conditions when granting such extensions to ensure the project remains consistent with current standards.

Design Drawing Requirements

Design drawings communicate the physical layout and details of the project. They must be clear, complete, and professional so that builders, reviewers, and inspectors can understand exactly how the project will be constructed.

Typical design drawings include the following information:

All details and specifications needed to construct every project component, in the correct location and orientation.

Demonstration that the design meets applicable codes, regulations, and design standards.

A location plan showing the position of the water system within its surroundings.

A service area map defining the system boundaries and identifying major components.

Project name and name of the legal water system owner.

Scale, north arrow, and date of preparation.

Name, address, and contact information for the design engineer or consulting firm.

A revision block showing dates and initials of the design engineer and drafter.

The engineer’s professional stamp and signature, as required by engineering registration laws (for example, WAC 246-290-040).

Locations of easements, rights-of-way, and property lines within the project area.

Locations of existing utilities and structures, both above and below ground, within the project impact area.

The 100-year flood elevation, if applicable, and protective design measures for facilities such as wells, pump stations, reservoirs, and treatment plants.

Applicable seismic design standards for the facility’s location.

Project Specifications

While drawings show what will be built, project specifications explain how it will be built and tested. Specifications complement the drawings by describing materials, equipment, installation methods, and performance requirements.

A complete set of specifications typically includes:

All information necessary to describe the means, methods, and standards for purchasing, installing, and testing each component.

Project name and legal owner (municipality, association, or individual).

Date of preparation and design engineer’s contact information.

The stamp and signature of the responsible design engineer.

A requirement for the contractor to submit shop drawings for review by the owner and engineer before construction.

Detailed descriptions of equipment start-up, disinfection, and inspection procedures, including acceptance testing. (Product cut-sheets do not replace full technical specifications.)

A description of the methods for maintaining water service during construction, if applicable.

Certification that all materials in contact with potable water meet ANSI/NSF Standard 61 for drinking water safety.

Contractor-Supplied Design Components

In some cases, certain parts of a project are provided as pre-engineered or packaged systems by the contractor—such as modular pump stations or prefabricated treatment units. When this occurs, the construction documents prepared by the design engineer must clearly indicate that the contractor will submit additional design drawings and specifications for review and approval before construction begins.

These contractor-supplied design documents are still considered engineering documents, and jurisdictions generally apply the same professional review and stamping requirements as for the original engineer-prepared drawings.

Common examples of contractor-supplied design components include:

Reservoirs

Skid-mounted pump stations

Packaged water treatment plants

Why Construction Documents Matter

Learning how to prepare clear and accurate construction documents helps young engineers connect design theory to real-world application. These documents bridge the gap between what engineers design and what contractors build.

By developing strong documentation habits early—consistent notation, organized drawings, and precise specifications—students strengthen skills related to technical communication, team coordination, and professional responsibility. These competencies directly support ABET outcomes in design, communication, and ethical practice, preparing you for upper-level courses, internships, and professional licensure.

Summary

The design of drinking water supply and treatment systems must balance multiple hydraulic and operational considerations to ensure efficient, safe, and sustainable water delivery. Design guides and manuals provide critical frameworks that ensure compliance with regulatory standards and best practices, while hydraulic analysis ensures that systems are capable of delivering water efficiently to all users. Through careful planning and adherence to established design principles, engineers can create robust drinking water systems that serve communities for decades to come.
